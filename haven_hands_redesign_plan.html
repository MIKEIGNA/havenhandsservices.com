What's wrong — ranked by impact
01
The splash screen must go
A loading animation with a progress bar before homepage content is an SaaS app pattern — not a domestic staffing agency in Nairobi. It delays trust and signals "this was built with a template." Both Msichana and Nestara load directly into the page with no intro animation.
Highest impact
02
The hero gallery stack is exaggerated decoration
Three overlapping rotated image cards with badge overlays and slide dots reads as a tech startup landing page, not a Nairobi service business. Msichana puts staff profile cards front and center — functional, not decorative. Nestara uses no hero image at all, just strong copy. Replace with one clean, real photo or a simple two-column text + image layout.
Highest impact
03
The Swahili headline is the right instinct, wrong execution
"Karibu — tafuta mtu sahihi kwa nyumba yako" in the hero is genuinely good — but it sits in isolation. Everything else on the page is English. This reads as pasted-on cultural flavoring rather than authentic bilingualism. Either commit (more Swahili throughout — WhatsApp greetings, service names, footer tagline) or use English with warmth naturally ("Welcome — find the right person for your home" and weave Swahili into copy organically like Msichana does).
Medium impact
04
Section pill badges make every section feel like a SaaS feature list
Badges like "Our Curriculum", "How It Works", "What People Say" floating in small teal pill shapes above every heading is a design tic from startup landing page templates. Neither Msichana nor Nestara use them. The heading itself carries the weight. Removing these will immediately make the page feel less generic.
Medium impact
05
Numbered training pillars 01–04 mimic a startup feature grid
Large "01, 02, 03, 04" numbers before each training area (Professional Communication, Housekeeping, First Aid, Childcare) is a direct copy of the SaaS "feature section" template pattern. For a domestic staffing service, this reads as corporate and cold. Nestara describes their values in flowing prose. Msichana lists services plainly with no decorative numbering. Replace with a clean card list or prose paragraph.
Medium impact
06
Hero typewriter animation reduces trust, not builds it
The rotating subtitle text that types and erases itself ("Trusted Nannies in Nairobi → Verified House Helps → ...") is a visual trick that delays the actual message. Nestara's hero says one thing confidently: "Your Circle of Care." Msichana says one thing: here are real people available now. A single, static sentence that tells families exactly what you do and how fast will outperform an animation every time.
Low impact
07
Hero background gradient makes it look like a healthcare product
The teal gradient wash across the hero section (--grad-hero: linear-gradient from #f0f7f7 to #e0f0f1) is pleasant but generic. Your teal #006A71 is strongest as a button color or accent — not as an atmospheric background. Both reference sites use clean white or near-white backgrounds throughout, making the brand color pop when it does appear. Switch the hero background to pure white (#ffffff) or warm off-white (#fafafa).
Low impact
What to keep — these are already working
Color palette ✓ Keep as-is
Your teal #006A71 is distinctive, professional, and ownable. Uncommon enough that it doesn't read as generic green. Use it for buttons, links, nav, and key accents — just not as a full background wash.
Typography ✓ Keep as-is
Outfit (headings) + Inter (body) is a strong, contemporary pair. The fonts are not the problem — the over-styling around them is. Keep these, but reduce heading weights from 800 to 600–700 for a less aggressive feel.
Outfit — clean, modern
Inter — readable, neutral body text that pairs well
Page architecture ✓ Keep as-is
Hero → How it works → Training → Staff → Testimonials → Pricing → CTA is a solid, proven flow for a staffing service. The structure is right — it's the visual execution that's too heavy. Don't restructure, simplify.
SEO + Nairobi signals ✓ Keep + extend
Schema markup, geo meta tags (KE, Nairobi, GPS coordinates), WhatsApp integration, and the Swahili hero copy are genuinely smart SEO and trust moves. Keep all of these and extend them — add more neighborhood names, Swahili content, and local references throughout.

Nairobi-specific improvements to add
M-Pesa payment mention
Msichana has an entire article about M-Pesa domestic payments. Add a single line in your pricing section: "Payment via M-Pesa or bank transfer accepted." This immediately signals local relevance and removes a friction point for potential clients.
Nairobi neighborhood targeting
Add "Areas we serve: Westlands, Karen, Kilimani, Runda, Lavington, Muthaiga, Parklands, Spring Valley, Gigiri, Kileleshwa." These are the families searching for domestic staff online. Msichana uses these keywords in article titles for SEO — you should too.
Real testimonials, Nestara-style
Nestara's testimonials are 5–6 sentences with real emotions ("I missed her first steps..."). Your current testimonials are short and generic. Ask 3 satisfied clients for a longer story. Use their first name and neighborhood: "Mary N., Lavington." This is the single biggest trust signal on any service site.
WhatsApp as the primary CTA
Both Msichana and Nestara treat WhatsApp as the main contact path — not a form. In Nairobi, WhatsApp is how business is done. Make your +254118971254 wa.me link the most prominent button on every page. The "Contact" form is secondary.
More authentic copy tone
Currently your copy uses phrases like "Unlock the care your family deserves" — Nestara-adjacent but slightly generic. Look at how Msichana writes: plain, direct, human. "Find a housegirl in Nairobi." "Hire a trained nanny this week." Confident simplicity builds more trust than aspirational language.
Staff profile page improvement
Msichana's housegirl profiles with photos, experience, and location are their entire product. Your workers.html should be the most polished page on the site — real photos, clear availability, experience highlights, neighborhood preference. That's what converts a visitor into a client.

The core principle of this redesign
Your current site tries to look impressive. The redesign should try to look trustworthy.

Msichana and Nestara succeed because they feel like you're talking to a real person in Nairobi who can solve your problem — not looking at a product page. They use plain language, real names, local neighborhood references, and WhatsApp as the primary contact method.

Your color scheme and font choice are already good. Your content (training program, vetting process, staff profiles) is genuinely strong. The problem is that it's all wrapped in design flourishes that signal "template" and "generic startup" rather than "trusted local service in Nairobi."

Strip the decoration. Let the content breathe. Make WhatsApp the primary CTA. Name the neighborhoods you serve. And get those long, human testimonials. That's the redesign.
